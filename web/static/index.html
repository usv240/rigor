The problem

Peer review can't catch what it never recomputes

Reviewers read for ideas and argument, not arithmetic. Almost nobody re-derives a single p-value by hand. So errors slip through, all the way to publication.

😵

The math goes unchecked

A reviewer might spend hours on a paper and never recompute one statistic. There isn't time, and it isn't what review is for.

🔒

The tools are locked away

Fraud-screening systems exist, but they are expensive, closed, sold to publishers, and they run after you submit. No help for the author beforehand.

📉

The cost is real

An honest slip can mean a desk rejection, a failed replication, or, at worst, a retraction that follows a career for years.

The process

What happens when you hit "check"

Four stages. Only one uses AI, and it never decides anything. Every verdict is arithmetic, so it cannot be hallucinated.

1

Read the paper

You paste text or drop a PDF. Rigor pulls out the prose, focusing on the Results section where the statistics live. Nothing is stored.

2

Extract the numbers i

A Qwen language model turns messy sentences like t(48) = 1.90, p < .001 into structured data: the test, its statistic, degrees of freedom, the reported p-value, the sample size, and the claim each result supports.

3

Recompute with exact math i

For every extracted result, Rigor recomputes the true p-value from the test statistic, checks each mean with the GRIM test, and cross-checks the degrees of freedom against the stated sample size. This is pure arithmetic.

4

Report, grounded in the math

You get a scored integrity report: each finding located, colour-coded by severity, and explained in plain language. Provable errors are separated from softer, AI-flagged judgement calls.

The four checks

What Rigor looks for

Two are established, peer-reviewed methods. Two go further than any tool we know of. All four are grounded in math, not opinion.

1. p-value recomputation i

From a test statistic and its degrees of freedom i, the true p-value is fixed. If the printed value disagrees, that is an error, and if it flips the significance decision, it is a serious one.

t(48) = 1.90, reported p < .001
recomputed p = 0.063 : not significant.

2. GRIM test i

The mean of N integer responses can only take certain values. Some reported means are arithmetically impossible: a fingerprint of a typo, a miscalculation, or fabrication.

M = 3.45, N = 10
impossible : nearest achievable are 3.4 and 3.5.

3. df vs N cross-check unique i

A test's degrees of freedom imply a minimum sample size. If a paper reports a df that needs more subjects than the study collected, the numbers contradict each other.

t(48) needs N ≥ 49, but the study reports N = 10.
You cannot analyse more cases than you have.

4. Claim vs evidence unique i

Does the conclusion actually follow from the data? Rigor flags claims of a significant effect for a result that recomputed as non-significant, plus causal or over-general wording.

Abstract: "significantly improved"
but the cited result recomputed to p = 0.06 : unsupported.

One paragraph, four problems

Here is what Rigor does with a normal-sounding Results paragraph. Every flag below is produced automatically.

In Study 1 (N = 10), the treatment group rated the target higher (M = 3.45, SD = 0.82) than controls. A paired t-test confirmed a significant effect, t(48) = 1.90, p < .001. In sum, this brief intervention significantly improves evaluations, showing that it causes more favourable judgments across the general population.

FAQ

Straight answers

Is this just a ChatGPT wrapper?

No. The language model's only job is to read the paper and transcribe the numbers. Every verdict is produced by deterministic math (exact distributions, arithmetic checks) that cannot be hallucinated. Delete the AI and you still have a correct statistics engine; you just lose the ability to read messy prose automatically.

Is my paper stored or used to train anything?

Rigor itself stores nothing: the text is checked in memory and discarded. Extraction is done by calling the Qwen API, so the text is processed by that service to return the structured numbers. For unpublished, sensitive work, self-host the open-source code and point it at your own endpoint.

What can Rigor NOT catch?

It checks the statistics a paper reports. It cannot detect fabricated raw data that is internally consistent, manipulated images, or plagiarism. It flags inconsistencies for a human to review; it does not accuse anyone of misconduct.

Which tests does it support?

p-value recomputation for t, F, chi-square, r, and z tests. The GRIM check for reported means of integer scales. The df-vs-N cross-check for t-tests and correlations. Claim-vs-evidence for any result it verified.

How accurate is it?

The deterministic checks are exact by construction. The only variable is extraction, so we benchmark the whole pipeline end to end: 100% detection and 0% false positives on a balanced 12-case set. That is a proof of concept, not a corpus, and scaling it is ongoing.

Is it free?

Yes. Rigor is free and open source under the MIT license. See the code.

Honest limitations

What to keep in mind

Rigor is an assistant, not a judge. Always review its flags yourself.

  • Extraction depends on the model reading correctly. A misread number can cause a missed or spurious flag, so treat findings as leads to verify, not verdicts.
  • The df-vs-N check uses one stated sample size. Papers with several studies or subsamples can be over-flagged; we deliberately only flag the impossible direction to limit this.
  • Claim-vs-evidence has two tiers. "Error" flags contradict a math-verified result and are provable. "Review" flags (causal or over-general language) are AI-assisted and need your judgment.
  • The benchmark is small. 12 curated cases demonstrate the pipeline; a large real-paper evaluation is future work, and we say so rather than overclaim.
